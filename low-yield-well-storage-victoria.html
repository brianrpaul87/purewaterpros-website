Low well recovery

Storage and treatment for low-yield wells.

A well that recovers slowly may supply enough water over a full day but not enough during showers, laundry and other peak demand. A properly planned storage system can separate slow recovery from household flow for homes in Victoria, Langford and surrounding Greater Victoria communities.

What the assessment considers

Storage planning considers

  • Measured well yield and recovery pattern
  • Household daily and peak demand
  • Reservoir, cistern or holding-tank volume
  • Level controls and dry-run protection
  • Booster delivery and treatment sequence

Practical system planning

Store water slowly, then deliver it when the home needs it.

An atmospheric storage tank or cistern can fill at a controlled rate that the well can sustain. A separate delivery system then supplies household pressure and peak flow. Treatment may be placed before storage, after storage or in stages depending on bacteria risk, sediment, iron, sulfur and maintenance needs.

Scope boundaryPure Water Pros focuses on water-treatment, storage and system-planning needs. Well drilling, well rehabilitation and regulated pump, electrical or plumbing work may require coordination with the appropriate qualified trades.

Reservoir sizing

Estimate usable storage around well recovery, household demand and the desired reserve.

Controlled filling

Use level controls and appropriate protection so the source well is not repeatedly overdrawn.

Holding tanks and cisterns

Plan accessible, cleanable storage with overflow, venting and service considerations.

Booster delivery

Coordinate repressurization equipment so the home receives stable pressure and flow.

Treatment placement

Decide whether filtration, chemical feed, UV or other stages belong before or after storage.

System coordination

Coordinate treatment with qualified pump, plumbing and electrical trades where their work is required.

How it works

A whole-system response to limited well recovery.

Equipment is selected only after the water, household demand, existing plumbing and maintenance expectations are understood.

  1. 1
    Confirm the limitation

    Review yield information, recovery behaviour, pressure symptoms and household demand.

  2. 2
    Plan usable storage

    Select realistic tank volume and filling strategy rather than relying on tank size alone.

  3. 3
    Coordinate delivery and treatment

    Arrange booster, controls, disinfection and filtration in a serviceable sequence.

  4. 4
    Commission and monitor

    Confirm fill behaviour, household delivery, treatment performance and cleaning requirements.
